Overview 📌


FEATURE

background

While Chemistry42 is a software platform for de novo small molecule design and optimization that integrates AI techniques with computational and medicinal chemistry methodologies, this case study will be focused on the experiment feature. Its main focus is to generate novel molecules based either on ligand or structure drug design.

my role

I worked collaboratively in a team with front-end and back-end developers, Product manager, and tester on the feature redesign. I contributed as a UX/UI Designer and was involved in the whole process of the experiment section development.

challenge

How to help clinical scientists in Pharma industry to efficiently find novel molecular structures against various biological targets?

goals

• Let scientists generate molecules using existing drug designs.
• Make molecular configuration and properties customizable according to users’ needs.
• Deliver diverse, high-quality molecular structures within hours.

Stakeholder Meeting 💬

Before conducting UX research, it was important to get a stakeholder’s brief first. As it turned out, the experiments feature was already implemented in the platform, however, stakeholders were not satisfied with it. During the meeting we were able to find out what was the problem with the current design, what exactly we were trying to achieve, and since the product is already out there whether there was any amount of data to work with already left.

Some of the outcomes that we got from the meeting as follows:

• The feature lacks a few important properties.
• The flow is confusing.
• The interface is not intuitive.
• There was no any use research conducted.

Research 🔍

interviews

"I need to generate an experiment from the existing one, but there is no such button." — Alexander M (Principal Scientist).

Because there was no initial user research conducted, we had to hear what our current users think about the feature. Although after stakeholder meeting we had already identified some general problems, we needed to make more detailed research to find specific pain points and improvement suggestions.

We started by conducting user interviews with 12 participants. The questions were focused on things the feature lacks, why it may be difficult use, what makes the flow unintuitive, as well as users’ goals. Also, we asked users to use the feature and comment on their every step.

affinity map

processing results

After analyzing the data and creating clusters, we got a clear understanding of the factors that prevent users from achieving their goals. That helped us to determine what must be improved, updated and added.

Eventually we came out with three main factors that had problems. They were visual design, UX design, and backend issues.

Persona 👤

After conducting user research and collecting the relevant information from each prospective user I created a user persona. To create the persona, we took into account goals, frustrations, background, and skills.

Ideation 💡

how might we…?

To encourage brainstorming and a change of perspective about the project, we tried rephrasing the problem statement, using a question by adding “How might we” at the beginning. From our research, we gained insights to create a Point-of-View from a more in-depth understanding of users, their needs and the most essential facts about them.

solutions

We provided a broad with enough scope to start thinking about solutions that go beyond the status quo. The team participated in a brainstorming session to answer the proposed question. We included each member's ideas about features of the future app so that everyone could vote for the most viable ones.

Task Flow ⚙️

To start redesigning the generation feature, we had to create a diagram that would represent a user's task flow. We have made a general flow showing all possible steps a user may take starting from generation creation until getting results and insights.

Wireframes ✏️

The next step was creating wireframes which would demonstrate where interface elements would exist and how they would roughly look. The process provided a blueprint of pages structure, layout, information and functions. This in turn would gives us a clear idea about the functioning and visual representation of the new feature.

Styleguide 🎨

brand

type

icons

colors

controls

Incorporating the principles of Gestalt, this project's style guide presents a streamlined and user-centric aesthetic. Using a minimalist approach enhances focus on key information while facilitating an intuitive understanding of content relationships. The design further includes modified icons from the Streamline library, ensuring a visually cohesive and intuitive user experience. The chosen monochromatic color scheme, punctuated with bright accents, increases contrast and improves readability.

Final UI 🌟

To mitigate cognitive load in order to maximize usability, we designed an interface that prioritized information using monochromatic components, which allowed us to reduce decision-making timing, thus highlighting priority content. The layout design was revolved around requiring a minimal amount of attention from the user, preventing the user from feeling overwhelmed with information.

Usability Testing 📈

For the usability test, we selected 6 research scientists of different gender and ages. We asked them to perform several tasks including creating and running new experiment and setting up specific properties, pausing/resuming generation, and exporting results.

outcomes

After conducting usability testing with each respondent, we have made a few usability improvements based on how users performed tasks as well as their feedback such as:

1. Option to rename experiment at any state instead of only the main page.
2. Saving all the settings and further uploading them as preset template.
3. Searching and filtering molecules by structure
