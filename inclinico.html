DASHBOARD

background

inClinico is a platform that provides data about targets, diseases, clinical trials, and scientists involved with the study at the preclinical and clinical stages.
This case study will be focused on the database dashboard, the main goal of which is to present information about past clinical trials.

my role

I worked collaboratively in a team with front-end and back-end developers, a product manager, and a tester. I contributed as a UX/UI designer and was involved in the whole design process of the dashboard.

challenge

How can we help clinical scientists from the Pharma industry to identify red flags in ongoing clinical trials and suggest corrections to eliminate the red flags before the first patient is enrolled?

interviews

I asked a series of questions to multiple users of the platform to help me learn more about what they would expect from the database dashboard as well as what they think about the current sources they use. The survey included a mix of open-ended and closed questions.

Overall the surveys were answered by 11 participants. The results that I got helped me to identify potential content and features of the future dashboard more precisely.

Styleguide üé®

brand

type

icons

colors

Incorporating the principles of Gestalt, this project's style guide presents a streamlined and user-centric aesthetic. Using a minimalist approach enhances focus on key information while facilitating an intuitive understanding of content relationships. The design further includes icons, ensuring a visually cohesive and intuitive user experience. The chosen monochromatic color scheme, punctuated with bright accents, increases contrast and improves readability.

Final UI üåü

To mitigate cognitive load in order to maximize usability, I designed an interface that prioritized information using monochromatic components, which allowed users to reduce decision-making timing, thus highlighting priority content. The layout design revolved around requiring a minimal amount of attention from users, preventing them from feeling overwhelmed with information.

Usability Testing üìà

For the usability test, we selected 6 research scientists of different genders and ages. Although the dashboard had basically one main purpose which was searching for past clinical trials, each was given a group of tasks that only varied in filtering options (i.e. chose specific start/end year, pick specific therapeutic area, etc.).

outcomes

After conducting usability testing with each respondent, we found one minor element that was eventually removed. That was a ‚Äúhigh-confidence only‚Äù switch in the predictions section. Because every research scientist expects to see the most precise prediction statistics, nobody would be interested in a less informative one, thus never using the switch.
